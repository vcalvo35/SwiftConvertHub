Cooking Measurement Systems Explained

Recipes around the world use three different measurement systems: the US customary system (cups, tablespoons, teaspoons, fluid ounces), the metric system (milliliters, liters, grams, kilograms), and the Imperial system (UK fluid ounces, which differ slightly from US). Understanding these differences is essential when adapting recipes across countries.

Volume: US Cups vs Metric Cups

One of the most common sources of confusion in international recipes is the cup. A US customary cup equals 236.588 mL (typically rounded to 240 mL), while an Australian or metric cup equals exactly 250 mL. That is a difference of about 13 mL per cup — small for one cup, but significant when a recipe calls for 4 cups of flour. Canadian recipes generally use the metric cup (250 mL); UK recipes use milliliters or Imperial fluid ounces rather than cups altogether.

US Volume Measurement Hierarchy

The US system has a neat hierarchy worth memorizing: 1 US cup = 16 tablespoons = 48 teaspoons = 8 fluid ounces = 236.588 mL. A tablespoon equals 3 teaspoons or 14.787 mL. A teaspoon is approximately 4.929 mL. These relationships make it easy to scale recipes up or down without a converter — half a cup is 8 tablespoons, a quarter cup is 4 tablespoons, and so on.

Weight in the Kitchen: Grams vs Ounces

Professional bakers and recipe developers overwhelmingly prefer grams over cups and ounces because weight is unambiguous — 200 g of flour is always 200 g regardless of how you spoon it into a container. Volume-based measurements for dry ingredients suffer from a packing problem: a cup of all-purpose flour measured by dipping into the bag can weigh 160 g, while a cup scooped lightly with a spoon levels out to around 120 g. That 33% variance is why professional recipes specify weight.

For those still working with US ounces: 1 ounce equals exactly 28.3495 grams (avoirdupois). A pound equals 16 ounces or 453.592 grams. For typical baking quantities, the conversion table below covers the most useful ranges.

Practical Tips for Converting Recipes

When adapting a US recipe for a metric kitchen, the first step is to identify which cup standard the recipe uses — always assume 237 mL (US cup) unless the recipe explicitly states "metric cup" or comes from Australia or New Zealand. For flour, sugar, and other dry ingredients, it is better to weigh in grams rather than trust a volume conversion, since the density of dry ingredients varies by how tightly packed they are. For liquids — water, milk, oil, vinegar — volume conversions are reliable: 1 US cup of water = 236.6 mL ≈ 237 g.

A practical shortcut for rounding: for most liquid ingredient conversions, 1 US cup ≈ 240 mL (close enough for home cooking). For precision baking — bread, pastry, soufflé — use the exact factor of 236.588 mL or simply weigh your liquids.

Common Baking Substitutions by Weight

Different baking ingredients have very different densities. Here are the standard weights for common ingredients when measured in 1 US cup: all-purpose flour is approximately 125 g (when spooned and leveled), granulated white sugar is 200 g, brown sugar (packed) is 220 g, butter is 227 g, honey is 340 g, cocoa powder is 85 g, and milk or water is approximately 240 g. These values are accepted industry standards used by USDA and leading culinary schools; they may vary slightly by brand or measurement technique.

The US vs UK Fluid Ounce Difference

A US fluid ounce equals 29.5735 mL. A UK (Imperial) fluid ounce equals 28.4131 mL. The difference is about 4%. This matters when a UK cookbook uses "fluid ounce" — always check the country of origin. Most modern UK recipes use milliliters rather than fluid ounces to avoid this ambiguity. Our converter handles both US and UK fluid ounces as separate units.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many mL are in a US cup?

A US customary cup equals 236.588 mL, often rounded to 237 mL or 240 mL in recipes. It is defined as exactly 8 US fluid ounces. Note that an Australian or metric cup equals 250 mL, which is slightly larger.

How many tablespoons are in a cup?

There are exactly 16 US tablespoons in 1 US cup. Each tablespoon is 3 teaspoons or 14.787 mL. So 1 cup = 16 tablespoons = 48 teaspoons = 8 fluid ounces = 236.588 mL.

How many grams are in an ounce?

One avoirdupois ounce equals exactly 28.3495 grams. This is the standard ounce used in kitchen recipes. (The troy ounce — used for precious metals — is 31.1035 g and is not used in cooking.)

Is a fluid ounce the same as a weight ounce?

No — they are different units. A fluid ounce (fl oz) measures volume (29.57 mL). A weight ounce (oz) measures mass (28.35 g). For water they are nearly equal (1 fl oz water ≈ 29.57 g), but for denser or lighter ingredients the difference is significant. Baking recipes that say "2 oz flour" mean weight ounces, not fluid ounces.

How do I convert a US recipe to metric?

For liquids: multiply US cups by 236.6 to get mL. For dry ingredients: use ingredient-specific weight equivalents rather than volume (e.g., 1 cup all-purpose flour = 125 g). This avoids packing inconsistency. For temperatures: use the formula °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9, or use our temperature converter.
